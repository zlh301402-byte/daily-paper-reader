# Wind-Resilient Trajectory Optimization for UAV-BS Networks: TD3 for Continuous Service Availability 

Azim Akhtarshenas ∗ German Svistunov ∗, Kuangyu Zheng ‡, and David L´ opez-P´ erez †∗Universitat Polit` ecnica de Val` encia (UPV), Spain 

†Beihang Valencia Polytechnic Institute (BVPI), China

Abstract —Unmanned aerial vehicle (UAV)-mounted base sta-tions are highly susceptible to wind disturbances such as gusts and turbulence, which induce positional drift and degrade com-munication link quality, particularly in emergency scenarios. To address this challenge, we propose a DRL-based framework for wind-resilient trajectory adjustment and positioning based on the Twin Delayed Deep Deterministic Policy Gradient (TD3) algorithm. The method models wind as a stochastic kinematic perturbation, avoiding complex aerodynamic modeling, thereby enabling the TD3 agent to learn adaptive control policies that maintain optimal coverage footprints. By prioritizing user-centric performance metrics under turbulent conditions, the proposed ar-chitecture ensures continuous service availability despite external disruptions. Simulation results demonstrate that the TD3-based approach effectively compensates for wind-induced displacements and outperforms benchmark methods, including Proximal Policy Optimization (PPO), in terms of throughput stability and robust-ness in windy environments. 

Index Terms —UAV Base Stations, Wind Turbulence Compensa-tion, TD3, Deep Reinforcement Learning 

I. I NTRODUCTION 

Unmanned aerial vehicles (UAVs) are increasingly deployed as aerial base stations in wireless communication systems [1], offering flexible and on-demand connectivity, particularly in emergency scenarios. However, their performance is signif-icantly degraded by environmental disturbances, especially wind and atmospheric turbulence. These disturbances induce trajectory deviations, instability, and reduced communication quality, thereby necessitating robust control and adaptation strategies [2]. Recent studies have highlighted the critical impact of atmospheric conditions on unmanned aerial vehicle-mounted base station (UAV-BS) systems. Surveys on wind-aware simulations and UAV-based atmospheric sensing further underscore the need for accurate turbulence modeling in real-istic UAV-BS deployments [3], [4].

A. Related Work 

Substantial efforts have investigated UAV-BS deployment and trajectory optimization from a communication perspective, targeting network metrics such as coverage, throughput, and fairness. However, most of these works assume ideal flight conditions and do not explicitly consider wind-induced dis-turbances. At the same time, extensive research has addressed wind-effect modeling and robust disturbance rejection to en-hance UAV-BS trajectory performance in turbulent environ-ments. Wind-resilient UAV-BS trajectory control methods can be broadly categorized as follows: 

1) Classical Wind-Resilient UAV-BS Trajectory Control: 

Significant efforts have been devoted to improving UAV-BSs stability and control under turbulent conditions. Traditional proportional-integral-derivative controllers, despite their sim-plicity, often exhibit degraded performance in the presence of nonlinear disturbances. In contrast, advanced model-based techniques such as cascaded model predictive control have demonstrated superior stability and disturbance rejection ca-pabilities [5]. Pilot-in-the-loop simulations have been used to assess controllability limits and highlight the sensitivity of UAV-base stations (BSs) to atmospheric turbulence [2]. Robust control methods, including sliding mode control methods [6], disturbance-rejection-based trajectory tracking [7], and fixed-time control [8], have shown enhanced performance under uncertainties and external disturbances. Trajectory adaptation strategies such as dual-mode control further enable UAVs to adjust motion according to wind conditions while preserving control authority in extreme environments [9]. Nevertheless, these approaches generally rely on accurate system dynamics and predefined assumptions. Consequently, their effectiveness diminishes under highly dynamic and stochastic wind con-ditions. Moreover, their high computational complexity and limited adaptability hinder real-time applicability in UAV tra-jectory optimization. 

2) DRL-Based Wind-Resilient UAV-BS Trajectory Control: 

To address high computational complexity and limited adapt-ability under severe wind turbulence, deep reinforcement learn-ing (DRL) has emerged as a promising paradigm for manag-ing wind disturbances, aerodynamic uncertainties, and multi-

For instance, DRL-based UAV-BS flight control using the Twin Delayed Deep Determin-istic Policy Gradient (TD3) and proximal policy optimization (PPO) algorithms was investigated in [11], where TD3 showed superior stability and faster convergence compared with PPO. Focusing on TD3, Himanshu et al. [12] reported improved navigation performance for quadrotors under disturbances, while [13] achieved robust stabilization of a twin-rotor sys-tem in windy conditions. Nevertheless, these studies primarily address low-level flight dynamics and navigation tasks. Exist-ing works typically treat UAV-BS control and communication performance independently, with limited integration of wind-resilient control strategies and communication-aware trajectory optimization in UAV-BS networks. 

B. Motivation and Contributions 

Despite recent advances in wind-resilient UAV-BS control, classical approaches [2], [5]–[9] rely on accurate system models and predefined dynamics, limiting their adaptability to highly dynamic and stochastic wind conditions. Although DRL-based solutions [11]–[13] have shown promising results, most prior works focus on flight stability and navigation, with limited emphasis on communication-aware trajectory op-timization. In contrast, our prior work [1] addressed UAV-BS positioning without accounting for wind effects or turbulence. Motivated by this gap, particularly in emergency scenarios requiring reliable connectivity under rapidly changing con-ditions, user mobility, and incomplete system information, we propose a DRL-based framework for joint wind-resilient trajectory control and positioning. The main contributions of this work are listed as follows:  

> •

We develop a TD3-based framework for adaptive UAV-BS positioning that captures UE mobility patterns, en-hances coverage performance, and improves fairness-aware throughput in dynamic environments.  

> •

We enable wind-resilient UAV-BS operation in GPS-denied scenarios by exploiting user equipment (UE) ref-erence signals and angle-of-arrival (AoA) measurements, improving robustness under turbulent conditions.  

> •

Simulation results demonstrate that the proposed TD3-based approach achieves faster convergence and outper-forms PPO in terms of mean reward and throughput. II. A TMOSPHERIC WIND DISTURBANCE MODELING

A. Simulation Configuration 

The simulation setup is described as follows: 

Network Settings: We consider a rectangular deployment area of 400 m × 1200 m , symmetric about the origin, with three hotspots initially located at (−350 , −550) , (350 , 0) , and 

(−350 , 550) (all coordinates in meters), following linear UE mobility. Each hotspot serves 10 UEs, deployed at a fixed altitude of 1.5 m and moving at a constant speed of 10 m /s.The hotspots move along straight lines from right to left and reverse direction upon reaching the deployment boundaries. The environment includes three UAV-BSs operating at an altitude of 100 m , each with a mass of 2.5 kg and an initial speed of 10 m /s. The maximum displacement per time step is limited to rmax = 20 m . Each UAV-BS operates with a system bandwidth of 5 MHz , a carrier frequency of 2 GHz , a transmit power of 43 dBm , and a noise power is −114 dBm /MHz . The propagation scenario is based on the 3GPP UMa model (TR 36.814), with omnidirectional antennas and Rician fading. 

Wind Disturbance Settings: Low-altitude wind is modeled as a composite process including constant, stochastic, shear, and gust components. The steady component has an average speed of 6.5 m /s, representing typical operating conditions. Stochastic turbulence is modeled using an autoregressive AR(1) process with a standard deviation of 1.5 m /s, capturing short-term fluctuations. A slowly varying shear component with am-plitude 1.0 m /s and a period of 180 steps accounts for gradual atmospheric variations over time. In addition, a transient gust event with a peak speed of 2.5 m /s is introduced at step 80 for a duration of 8 steps, modeling wind disturbances [2], [14]. 

Neural Network Settings: The main TD3 hyperparameters are defined as follows. Both the actor and critic networks use a constant learning rate of 3×10 −4 and consist of three hidden layers with 256 neurons each. The TD3 algorithm uses a replay buffer of size 10 6, a batch size of 256, a discount factor γ =0.99 , and a soft update coefficient τ = 0 .005 . Gradient clipping with a maximum norm of 1.0 is applied to stabilize training. In addition, delayed policy updates with a factor of 2, target policy smoothing with clipped noise ( σ = 0 .2, clip = 0 .5), and decaying exploration noise (initial standard deviation = 0 .2)are employed. For PPO, a constant learning rate of η = 3 ×

10 −5 is used, with both actor and critic networks having the same architecture of three hidden layers with 256 neurons each. PPO uses γ = 0 .99 and λ = 0 .95 to balance short- and long-term rewards. For both algorithms, training is performed over 46k episodes, consisting of 256 time-steps. 

B. Numerical Results 

To assess generalization, the performance of the proposed system is evaluated under two scenarios. During training, the initial UAV-BSs positions are randomly initialized, and the reward and throughput are monitored accordingly (See Fig. 2(a) and (b)). During evaluation, the UAV-BSs are initialized with two predefined positions at the altitude of 100 m and horizontal coordinates [(0 , 180) , (0 , 0) , (0 , −180)] for the first scenario, and [( −180 , 380) , (180 , 380) , (−180 , −380)] for the second scenario correspondingly. The system performance is assessed every 500 iterations (See Fig. 2(c) and (d)). Fig. 2 presents the simulation results evaluating both communication performance (throughput) and RL performance (reward). The figure com-pares off-policy TD3 and on-policy PPO under wind turbulence during the training phase (Fig. 2(a) and (b)) and the evaluation phase (Fig. 2(c) and (d)). During training, Fig. 2(a) shows that TD3 achieves a higher and more stable mean reward, converging to approximately 0.65, while PPO converges to a lower value with noticeable fluctuations. Fig. 2(b) further demonstrates that TD3 rapidly reaches a mean throughput of approximately 22 Mbps within the early training stages (around 

10 4 episodes), whereas PPO improves more gradually and ex-hibits higher variance. As shown in Fig. 2(c), TD3 consistently achieves higher reward values with lower variability, indicating more effective optimization of the fairness-based objective. Fig. 2(d) confirms that TD3 maintains a mean throughput of ap-proximately 22 Mbps across different initializations, while PPO converges to approximately 16 Mbps with increased variability. These results demonstrate that TD3 provides more robust and consistent performance under dynamic wind conditions. VI. C ONCLUSION 

This paper presented a TD3-based DRL framework for wind-resilient UAV-BS trajectory optimization. By modeling wind as a stochastic disturbance and incorporating communication-aware objectives, the proposed approach enables adaptive UAV-BS positioning while maintaining service continuity. Sim-ulation results demonstrate that TD3 consistently outperforms PPO in terms of convergence speed, stability, and communi-cation performance, achieving higher reward and throughput under varying wind conditions. These findings highlight the effectiveness of TD3 in handling dynamic and non-stationary environments. Overall, the proposed framework provides a practical and robust solution for UAV-BS-assisted emergency communications, particularly in challenging scenarios such as wind disturbances and GPS-denied environments.
